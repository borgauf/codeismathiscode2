An Intuitive Guide to the Dot Product

In the world of AI, the dot product is the primary tool used to figure out how much two things “align” with each other.

—

1. The Grocery Store Analogy (Weighted Sum)

Imagine you are at a fruit stand. You have two lists:

  1. The Price List (Vector A): Apples are \(2\), Bananas are \(1\), and Cherries are \(5\).
  2. Your Shopping Cart (Vector B): You bought \(3\) apples, \(10\) bananas, and \(0\) cherries.

The dot product is simply the total cost of your cart. You multiply the corresponding items and add them up: \[(2 \times 3) + (1 \times 10) + (5 \times 0) = 6 + 10 + 0 = 16\]

—

2. The Mathematical Definition

If you have two vectors \(A\) and \(B\) of the same length, the dot product (written as \(A \cdot B\)) is the sum of the products of their corresponding components:

\[A \cdot B = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + \dots + a_nb_n\]

—

3. Why It Matters: “Alignment”

The dot product tells you how much two vectors are “pointing” in the same direction:

  • High Positive Score: The vectors are very similar. They point in the same direction.
  • Zero Score: The vectors are “orthogonal” (at a \(90^\circ\) angle). They have nothing in common.
  • Negative Score: The vectors are opposites. They point in away from each other.

In AI, when we say a Query (\(Q\)) “attends” to a Key (\(K\)), we are literally just taking the dot product to see how well they match!

—

4. Scheme Code Example

Since Scheme is a functional language, we can calculate a dot product by multiplying the elements of two lists and then summing the results. 

(define (dot-product vec1 vec2)
  (if (null? vec1)
      0
      (+ (* (car vec1) (car vec2))
         (dot-product (cdr vec1) (cdr vec2)))))

;; Example Usage:
;; Vector A: (2 3 4)
;; Vector B: (1 0 2)
;; Calculation: (2*1) + (3*0) + (4*2) = 10

(display (dot-product '(2 3 4) '(1 0 2))) 
;; Output: 10

—

5. Geometric Perspective

If you prefer shapes, the dot product can also be calculated using the angle \(\theta\) between the two vectors and their lengths (magnitudes):

\[A \cdot B = \|A\| \|B\| \cos(\theta)\]

This confirms the “alignment” idea: if the angle \(\theta\) is \(0\), then \(\cos(0) = 1\), maximizing the score. If the angle is \(90^\circ\), then \(\cos(90^\circ) = 0\), making the score zero.

Summary: The dot product is a single number that summarizes how much two multi-dimensional objects overlap.
