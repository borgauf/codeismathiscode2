Category theory for projections

Following is a very math-dense definition of a projection

 A projection is a morphism

\begin{align*} \pi_i : \prod_{j∈J }X_j \to X_i \end{align*}

defined by the universal property of the product — specifically, it is the unique family of morphisms such that for any object \(T\) and any family of morphisms \(f_j : T → X_j\)​, the induced morphism \(\langle f_j \rangle : T → \prod X_j\) satisfies \(π_i \circ ⟨f_j⟩ = f_i\).

Can you break this down and explain this to a beginner?

Here be category theory

This definition comes from a branch of mathematics called Category Theory, which is famous for being incredibly abstract. Instead of looking at what objects are (like numbers, sets, or points), category theory defines objects purely by how they interact with other objects through “arrows” or mappings.

Here is a plain-English breakdown of what this definition is actually saying, piece by piece.

1. The Core Vocabulary

  • Morphism: This is just a generalized word for a mapping, function, or “arrow” that transforms one thing into another.

  • The Product (\(\prod_{j\in J} X_j\)): Imagine you have a collection of different objects (\(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(X_3\), etc.). The product is a new, combined object that packages all possible combinations of them together. For example, if \(X_1\) is a set of letters and \(X_2\) is a set of numbers, their product is the ordered set of all possible coordinate pairs, i.e., a sequence or a 2-tuple of elements, the first element coming from \(X_1\), the second from \(X_2\). This is actually the Cartesian product, and the \(\prod\) symbol, the product, produces. Let \( X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n \) be any sets. Their Cartesian product (the set of all n-tuples) is

    \begin{align*} \prod_{i=1}^{n} X_i = X_1 \times X_2 \times \dots \times X_n = \{ \langle x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \rangle \mid x_i \in X_i \} \end{align*} \begin{align*} \prod_{i=1}^{n} X_i = X_1 \times X_2 \times \dots \times X_n \end{align*}
  • The Projection (\(\pi_i\)): This is the function that extracts just one specific piece from that combined package. If you have a combined object \((x_1, x_2)\), the projection \(\pi_1\) just spits out \(x_1\).

2. The Universal Property (The “Kitchen” Analogy)

The second half of the definition explains the “Universal Property.” In category theory, this is the strict rule that proves the combined object you built is a true product.

Let’s use an analogy to understand the math symbols: Imagine \(X_1\) is Bread and \(X_2\) is Cheese. Their product (\(\prod X_j\)) is a Cheese Sandwich. The projection (\(\pi_1\)) is the action of pulling the bread off the sandwich.

Now, introduce a completely new object, \(T\). Let’s call \(T\) the Kitchen (a starting point).

  • \(f_1 : T \to X_1\) means you have a specific method (a morphism) to produce Bread from the Kitchen.
  • \(f_2 : T \to X_2\) means you have a specific method to produce Cheese from the Kitchen.

The definition states that if you have these individual methods, there must be a unique induced morphism \(\langle f_j \rangle : T \to \prod X_j\). In our analogy, \(\langle f_j \rangle\) is the unique, master recipe to build the entire Cheese Sandwich directly from the Kitchen.

3. The Final Equation Explained

The entire definition hinges on this final required condition: \[\pi_i \circ \langle f_j \rangle = f_i\]

The little circle (\(\circ\)) means “composition,” which just means doing one action after another. You read it from right to left.

So, \(\pi_1 \circ \langle f_j \rangle\) translates to:

  1. Use the master recipe to build the whole Cheese Sandwich from the Kitchen (\(\langle f_j \rangle\)).
  2. Then, use the projection to rip the Bread off the sandwich (\(\pi_1\)).

The equation states that doing those two steps must be exactly equal (\(=\)) to \(f_1\), which is the method of just making the Bread from the Kitchen in the first place.

If building the whole package and then extracting a piece gives you the exact same result as just building the piece directly, then your projection is mathematically valid!
