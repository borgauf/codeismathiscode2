* A Foundational Definition of Mathematical Logic

At its core, *mathematical logic is the formal, systematic study of
mathematical reasoning and truth.* Rather than treating logic as a
philosophical exercise in human argumentation, mathematical logic
treats logical frameworks themselves as mathematical objects to be
rigorously analyzed. It abstracts the intuitive processes of
mathematical deduction into formal, mechanical systems so that we can
prove theorems /about/ the systems themselves (metamathematics).

To fully grasp this, it is helpful to break logic down into the two
distinct realms it bridges:

** 1. Syntax (The Realm of Form)

Syntax deals exclusively with symbols and the mechanical rules for
manipulating them, devoid of any "meaning." A formal syntactic system
consists of:

- *An Alphabet:* A defined set of symbols (e.g., variables $x, y, z$,
  logical connectives $\land, \lor, \neg, \rightarrow$, and
  quantifiers $\forall, \exists$).
- *A Formal Language:* A strict set of grammatical rules that dictate
  which strings of symbols form "well-formed formulas" (WFFs),
  typically denoted by lowercase Greek letters like $\phi$ and $\psi$.
- *A Deductive System:* A chosen set of starting formulas (axioms) and
  mechanical rules of inference (like /modus ponens/: $\phi, \phi
  \rightarrow \psi \vdash \psi$) used to derive new formulas
  (theorems) from existing ones.

** 2. Semantics (The Realm of Meaning)

Semantics assigns meaning and truth values to the otherwise
meaningless symbols of the syntax.

- *Structures/Models:* A domain of discourse (like the set of natural
  numbers $\mathbb{N}$) equipped with specific relations, functions,
  and constants (e.g., a structure $\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathbb{N},
  +, \times, 0, 1, \leq \rangle$).
- *Truth:* A precise definition of what it means for a well-formed
  formula to be "true" within a given model, denoted by the
  satisfaction relation $\mathcal{M} \vDash \phi$.

** The Bridge: Soundness and Completeness

The primary goal of introductory mathematical logic is to study the
relationship between syntax (what can be /proved/, denoted by
$\vdash$) and semantics (what is /true/, denoted by $\vDash$).

- *Soundness:* If a formula can be proved using the syntactic
  deductive system, it must be true in all valid models. The system
  does not prove falsehoods. Formally: If $\vdash \phi$, then $\vDash
  \phi$.
- *Completeness:* If a formula is true in all valid models, there must
  exist a syntactic proof for it. The system is powerful enough to
  prove all universal truths. Formally: If $\vDash \phi$, then $\vdash
  \phi$.

** The Four Pillars

As the field expanded, this foundational study of formal systems grew
into four distinct, highly interconnected sub-disciplines:

1. *Model Theory:* The study of mathematical structures (groups,
   fields, graphs) through the lens of the formal languages used to
   describe them.
2. *Proof Theory:* The study of formal proofs themselves as
   mathematical objects, often focusing on their structure, length,
   and the consistency of deductive systems.
3. *Set Theory:* The study of collections of objects and the infinite,
   serving as the foundational language and ontology for nearly all of
   modern mathematics (e.g., the $ZFC$ axioms).
4. *Computability Theory (Recursion Theory):* The study of which
   mathematical problems can be solved (and which formulas can be
   decided) by an algorithm or a mechanical process, formalizing
   concepts like Turing machines and recursive functions.
