* Injective successor and its contrapositive

In first-order logic, the contrapositive of an implication $P \to Q$
is $\neg Q \to \neg P$.

Here is how that breaks down for the second Peano axiom:

- *Original Premise* ($P$): $S(x) = S(y)$
- *Original Conclusion* ($Q$): $x = y$
- *Implication* ($P \to Q$): $S(x) = S(y) \to x = y$

To form the contrapositive, /negate both statements and reverse the
direction of the implication/:

- *Negated Conclusion* ($\neg Q$): $x \ne y$ (which is shorthand for
  $\neg(x = y)$)
- *Negated Premise* ($\neg P$): $S(x) \ne S(y)$
- *Contrapositive* ($\neg Q \to \neg P$): $x \ne y \to S(x) \ne S(y)$

Because a conditional statement and its contrapositive are logically
equivalent, the axiom $\forall x \forall y (S(x) = S(y) \to x = y)$
can be perfectly swapped with $\forall x \forall y (x \ne y \to S(x)
\ne S(y))$ in any formal proof.

** Gentzen warmup: the turnstile: $\vdash$ in Sequent Calculus

In Gentzen's sequent calculus,the $\vdash$ symbol is called
*turnstile*, and it appears in expressions of the form:[fn:1]

$$\Gamma \vdash \Delta$$

This is called a *sequent*, and it has the following meaning:

@@html:<font color = "#0d3db3">@@ ⥤ If all the formulas in $\Gamma$
hold, then at least one formula in $\Delta$ holds.  @@html:</font>@@

*** The Components

- *$\Gamma$ (the antecedent)* --- a finite list of formulas representing
  /assumptions/ or /hypotheses/
- *$\Delta$ (the succedent/consequent)* --- a finite list of formulas
  representing /conclusions/
- *$\vdash$* --- separates the two sides, expressing a /derivability/ or
  /entailment/ relation between them

*** Intuitive Reading

| Sequent          | Reading                                                                 |
|------------------+-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| $A, B \vdash C$  | "From $A$ and $B$, we can derive $C$"                                   |
| $\vdash A$       | "$A$ is a tautology / provable with no assumptions"                     |
| $A \vdash$       | "$A$ is a contradiction (it entails anything, including the empty set)" |
| $\vdash$         | The empty sequent --- unprovable (deriving nothing from nothing)        |

*** Classical vs. Intuitionistic Reading (optional)

In *classical logic (LK)*, $\Delta$ may contain multiple formulas, and the
sequent $\Gamma \vdash \Delta$ means:

$$\bigwedge \Gamma \;\Rightarrow\; \bigvee \Delta$$

the conjunction of all hypotheses implies the /disjunction/ of all conclusions.

In *intuitionistic logic (LJ)*, $\Delta$ is restricted to /at most one/
formula, which forces a constructive reading: you must explicitly /produce/
the single conclusion, not merely rule out all alternatives.

*** Why it Matters

The turnstile is the engine of the calculus. Gentzen's key insight was to make
the /structure of proofs/ explicit by treating $\vdash$ not as a semantic truth
relation (as in model theory) but as a *syntactic derivability relation*, built
up step by step via inference rules (like /cut/, /weakening/, /contraction/,
and the introduction rules for each connective). His *Cut-Elimination Theorem*
(/Hauptsatz/) then shows that any proof can be transformed into one with no
"detours," giving logic a kind of canonical proof structure.

Now, is "logically entails" the right gloss for $\vdash$? It's close,
but worth being precise about the distinction --- it depends on
whether you're working *syntactically* or *semantically*.

Technically we want the /two turnstiles/ symbol.

*"Logically entails"* ($\models$) is the /semantic/ double-turnstile:
it means "in every model where $A$ is true, $B$ is also true." This is
a statement about /truth in interpretations/.

*$\vdash$* is the /syntactic/ single-turnstile: it means "there exists
a /proof/ of $B$ from $A$ using the inference rules." This is a
statement about /derivability in a formal system/.

- *$A \models B$* --- "$A$ logically entails $B$" / "$B$ is a semantic
  consequence of $A$"
- *$A \vdash B$* --- "$A$ proves $B$" / "$B$ is derivable from $A$" / "$B$
  is a syntactic consequence of $A$"

In practice, people do sometimes say "entails" loosely for $\vdash$, and in
systems that are *sound and complete* (like classical propositional logic),

$$A \vdash B \iff A \models B$$

so the distinction collapses extensionally. But conceptually they are different
things --- one lives in proof theory, the other in model theory --- and
Gentzen's sequent calculus is specifically about the /syntactic/ side. So
"proves" or "derives" is the more accurate gloss for $\vdash$.

** Gentzen Sequent Formulation

To formalize /why/ these are logically equivalent, we can use
Gentzen's sequent calculus (specifically, classical system LK). We
want to prove that the contrapositive follows from the original
implication:

$$P \to Q \vdash \neg Q \to \neg P$$

Letting $P$ represent $S(x) = S(y)$ and $Q$ represent $x = y$, the
derivation tree using standard left and right rules looks like
this

$$
\dfrac{
  \dfrac{
    \dfrac{
      \dfrac{ P \vdash P \qquad Q \vdash Q }{ P \to Q, P \vdash Q } ({\to}L)
    }{
      P \to Q, P, \neg Q \vdash
    } (\neg L)
  }{
    P \to Q, \neg Q \vdash \neg P
  } (\neg R)
}{
  P \to Q \vdash \neg Q \to \neg P
} ({\to}R)
$$

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of that derivation:
1. *Axioms*: We start with the identity sequents $P \vdash P$ and $Q
   \vdash Q$.
2. *Left Implication* (${\to}L$): We introduce the implication $P \to
   Q$ on the left side. This bridges our axioms, essentially stating
   that with $P \to Q$ and $P$, we can derive $Q$.
3. *Left Negation* ($\neg L$): We introduce $\neg Q$ to the left side
   context. Because we already have $Q$ on the right, this results in
   an empty right side (representing a contradiction or falsum).
4. *Right Negation* ($\neg R$): We resolve the contradiction by moving
   $P$ from the left context to the right side by negating it,
   yielding $\neg P$.
5. *Right Implication* (${\to}R$): Finally, we discharge the
   assumption $\neg Q$ from the left context to form the final
   implication $\neg Q \to \neg P$ on the right.

While the original implication is how the axiom is usually formalized,
the contrapositive is often the more intuitive way to think about what
the axiom actually /means/: *the successor function is injective
(one-to-one)*. It guarantees that two different numbers will always
have two different successors, ensuring the natural numbers stretch
out infinitely without ever looping back onto themselves.
