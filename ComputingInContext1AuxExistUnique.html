Existence and uniqueness

Apostol is leaning into the foundational Existence and Uniqueness property of the real number system. While it might seem like common sense—of course \(2 + 3\) is \(5\) — in the context of Tom Apostol’s rigorous approach, this statement is the first step in defining Binary Operations — which we’ll explore soon.

By starting here, Apostol is establishing the Field Axioms.

1. Addition and Multiplication as Functions

In higher mathematics, we view addition (\(+\)) and multiplication (\(\cdot\)) as functions that take an ordered pair of real numbers and map them to a single result. As mentioned earlier, functions in higher math have a more exacting definition which we’ll only briefly touch here.

Apostol is essentially saying:1 Quickly described, a binary operation is any operation on two things. Yes, but in grade school we added a whole column of numbers. No, you didn’t. Technically, you added two numbers, got an answer, then added the next number to that augend answer—over and over. This touches on the recursive nature of math lurking in so many places. We’ll have lots more fun with recursion later. (Perhaps see the Wikipedia on Addition for more detail about why adding is a binary operation.) , 2 Don’t worry about the tuple \((x,y)\). You’ve seen pairs (tuples) as points plotted on graphs, i.e., the first number represents the x-axis and the second number the y-axis. Here the tuple refers to the higher-math definition of a function where, as in the algebra graph example, a function pairs a number from the x-axis with a number from the y-axis. A binary operation like adding \(3\) and \(2\) would be represented as \((3,2)\).

  • Binary Operation: For every pair \((x, y)\) — or in regular arithmetic \(x + y\) — there is a result.
  • Well-defined: The result is unique. You will never perform \(x + y\) and get two different answers.

2. The Property of Closure

This assumption also implies closure. If \(x\) and \(y\) are real numbers, the unique result \(x + y\) is also a member of the set of real numbers. This ensures that when we do algebra we never “fall out of” or escape the set of real numbers into something undefined. In fancy math talk, the binary operation of addition is closed in the set of real numbers. Again, it just means when you add real numbers, you get a real number, not some surprise non-real number. This may seem the worst of hair-splitting, but consider \(\mathbb{N}\), the “natural” counting numbers \(1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots\) Now, we cannot say there is a closure on binary operation of subtraction, since, e.g., \(3 - 4\) is a negative number, i.e., outside the counting numbers, which are always positive.

Comparison with Other Operations

To see why uniqueness matters, consider operations that don’t always have this property in other contexts:

Operation Unique in \(\mathbb{R}\)? Reason
Addition (\(x + y\)) Yes Field Axiom 1
Multiplication (\(xy\)) Yes Field Axiom 1
Square Root (\(\sqrt{x}\)) No \(x^2 = 4\) has two solutions: \(2\) and \(-2\).
Division (\(x / y\)) Mostly Unique except when \(y = 0\) (undefined).

3. Why Bother Stating the Obvious?

It sets the stage for proving everything else. Without the guarantee of uniqueness:

  • Substitution would fail: If \(x + y\) could be two different things, you couldn’t reliably replace \(x + y\) with a single value in an equation.
  • Equivalence would break: We couldn’t say \(a = b \implies a + c = b + c\) with absolute certainty.

Later we’ll get into the most basic laws of arithmetic, namely Peano’s Axioms3 Imagine you meet seemingly advanced aliens who just landed in a fancy space ship. You start communications by writing numbers on a board, \(1,2,3,\ldots\), and they eventually understand, but smirk and act dismissive. Confused, you ask them to explain how they do simple numbers, “So tell us what how you would represent \(3\).” They write on the board \(3 = S(S(S(0)))\). They explain that \(S\) is a successor function that gives you “the next number,” starting with \(S(0) = 1\) … this is the basis of Peano established. Soon… and how everything builds up from that.
