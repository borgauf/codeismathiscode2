Graphs, search, state space

Basics of graph theory

In its most basic form a graph is nothing more than points and lines connecting these points. But of course the whole purpose of these points and connecting lines is to represent concepts. Below we see a section of a thematic map of the London subway system.

Being “thematic” means we aren’t supposed to see this map as a true and accurate map as the roughly same area depicted below

Instead, the London Underground map is meant to display only the relevant information of

  • subway stops
  • relative positions of the subway stops
  • rail lines running between subway stops

with no consideration of further accuracy or usage. Obviously, we’re in the realm of abstraction when we boil down a regular, reasonably accurate map into one of just points and lines. And yet even small children can grasp what is meant by Figure 1. Below is a thematic map depiction of the seven bridges of Königsberg, where graph theory began as Leonard Euler first abstracted the idea of bridges and land masses (two river banks and two islands in a river)

A graph is an ordered pair \(G = (V, E)\) where \(V\) is the set of vertices, and \(E ⊆ \{{x, y\} \; \vert \; x, y \in V, x \ne y}\) is a set of unordered pairs of vertices called edges.
