it’s computer science, not just computers


Raymond Smullyan

— Raymond Smullyan

Up front and brutal: Computer science is a branch of applied math. The main problem with this simple fact is, from comp-sci department to comp-sci department, to what degree, to what depth the math is taken can differ wildly. That is to say, at each place different aspects, corners of the applicable mathematics surrounding computer science will be different from the next in how they approach, delineate, present this “computer science” math. Most computer scientists we the beginning of modern computer science goes back to the mathematics of Alan Turing, Alonzo Church (a professor of Turing’s), Kurt GÃ¶del, and another student of Church’s, Stephen Kleene. Ironically Turing and Church were exploring the mathematical logic of computation back in the 1930s, long before any such device as a computer had been developed.1 …although many will now insist the collaboration of Ada Lovelace (pictured) and Charles Babbage on Babbage’s difference engine was the very start of modern computing. Lovelace is credited as the first to envision a computer programming language in 1843.

They were formalizing the general lore around David Hilbert’s Entscheidungsproblem (decision problem). Basically, it asks the very fundamental question, Can we figure stuff out? but of course in a very mathematically formal way using just logic.

The Entscheidungsproblem asks for an algorithm that considers an inputted statement and answers “yes” or “no” according to whether it is universally valid, i.e., valid in every structure.

But then what in this high-flying mathematical sense is an algorithm and what is a structure? We will pick these apart as we go.

Gottfried Leibniz is credited with getting this idea of translating all questions to math and then cranking some mathematical device to get an automated answer. Back in the 1600s he wondered whether a mechanical computing device (or equivalent mathematics) could ever boil all human thought down into math symbols and “process” them objectively

Leibniz’s idea of reasoning through a universal language of symbols and calculations remarkably foreshadows great 20th-century developments in formal systems, such as Turing completeness, where computation was used to define equivalent universal languages.

An example might be the infamous Collatz conjecture, which asks if any positive integer can be subjected to this (piecewise) function

\begin{align*} f(n) = \begin{cases} n/2, & \text{if $n$ is even} \\ 3n+1, & \text{if $n$ is odd} \end{cases} \end{align*}
  • If the number \(x\) is odd then perform \([3x + 1]\) successively until it is even (i.e., \(x \; mod \; 2 = 0\))
  • If the number \(x\) is even then perform \(\frac{x}{2}\) successively until it is odd

does the function eventually take the number down to \(1\)? For example, consider \(8\). Since \(8\) is even, we divide by \(2\) to get \(4\), which is even, so divide again by \(2\) to get %2%. Then even \(2\) is again divided by \(2\) to get %1%. Since \(1\) is odd we multiply by \(3\) and add \(1\) — which is \(4\)… And so we are in an infinite loop. But as the test asks, does \(8\) arrive after a certain number of applications to \(1\)? The answer is yes. And yet mathematicians have yet to prove if all numbers can, after a finite number of iterations, ever reach \(1\).

Not long ago a simple raise-to-the-power-of question like, e.g., \(53^{1000}\), could put a computer into an infinite loop or make a hand calculator simply reply gone fishin'. But with the modern programming language Racket we can do very large numbers indeed 2 By the way, \(53^{1000}\) is many times larger than the count of all the atoms in the universe.
