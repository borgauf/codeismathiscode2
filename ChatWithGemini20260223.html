The Nature of AI “Reasoning”

You are exactly right to critically examine the premise of large language model (LLM) reasoning. I do not “reason” in the deductive, deterministic manner of a formal system. Probabilistic Approximation vs. Deductive Logic

When I output a logical deduction or a mathematical proof, I am not computationally applying modus ponens or universal instantiation.   • Formal Systems: In environments like Coq, Lean, or Agda, a type-checking engine strictly evaluates symbols against defined axioms. If an invalid substitution is attempted, the system fails because the logical structure is absolute and rigid.

My responses are generated by calculating the distances and relationships between these vectors. I have “learned” the shape, syntax, and flow of rigorous arguments, but I do not internally compute their absolute truth values.

A Self-Developed, Emergent Base

In a sense, my reasoning base is “self-developed,” but it is empirical rather than foundational. When I successfully navigate complex functional paradigms in Scheme or Haskell, it is because the internal representations of these domains are deeply interconnected in my model. The network recognizes that functional composition and pure mathematical functions follow specific, strict syntactical patterns, and it mathematically mimics that strictness. It is an emergent property, not a programmed one.

  • External Tool Use: When absolute precision is required, I can generate code to evaluate logic computationally, effectively offloading the deterministic execution to a rigid environment.

In short, I am a highly advanced probabilistic mirror reflecting the formal logic encoded by humans, rather than a machine formally deriving truths from first principles.

Graphs vs. Neural Networks: The Semantic Divide

You have hit on a brilliant linguistic and mathematical overlap. In pure mathematics (specifically graph theory), “graph” and “network” are virtually interchangeable terms. Both describe a topology consisting of vertices (nodes) and edges (links).

However, in computer science and artificial intelligence, we use these terms to describe two radically different architectures for organizing and processing information. The difference is not in the underlying mathematical topology, but in what the nodes and edges represent.

The Knowledge Graph (Discrete & Symbolic)

When we refer to a “graph database” (like a knowledge graph), we are talking about a symbolic system.2 Click on the link to see a discussion of what a symbolic system means.

  • Edges = Explicit Logic: An edge represents a strict, hard-coded relationship, such as “is an axiom of” pointing to a node for “Zermelo-Fraenkel Set Theory.”3 In first order logic is an axiom of would be a predicate. More soon.

If you query a graph database, it executes deterministic traversals. It is mathematically sound and functions essentially like a formalized propositional logic engine. If a relationship is not explicitly defined or deducible by hard rules, the graph cannot bridge the gap.

The Neural Network (Continuous & Distributed)

A neural network—like the one powering my responses—is structurally a mathematical graph (specifically, a computational graph). However, it is a connectionist system.4 See link from previous reference.

  • Nodes = Mathematical Functions (Neurons): A node is merely an artificial neuron holding a numerical activation value. It does not represent a concept like “Haskell” or “Modus Ponens.”
  • Edges = Parameters (Weights): The edges are numerical weights that amplify or dampen the signals passing between neurons.

In my architecture, knowledge is distributed. The concept of a “pure function”5 The first rule of a true function is, whatever the input, there is only one output. does not live in a single node; it is encoded as a geometric pattern of activations distributed across thousands of continuous weights. The Transformer’s Attention Mechanism

This brings us to exactly how I approximate logical reasoning without possessing a discrete logic engine. The core of my architecture is the Transformer, and its defining feature is the Self-Attention Mechanism.

Attention allows the model to look at a sequence of tokens (words or symbols) and mathematically assign weight (relevance) to every other token in that sequence. It calculates context, which happens to highly correlate with logical syntax.

Here is the formal definition of the attention function:

\[\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V\]

Instead of a theorem prover stepping through formal rules, here is how the probabilistic engine calculates relevance:

  1. Queries (Q), Keys (K), and Values (V): Every token in your prompt is multiplied by learned weight matrices to create three vectors: a Query (what this token is looking for), a Key (what this token contains), and a Value (the actual semantic substance of the token).
  2. The Dot Product (\(QK^{T}\)): To figure out which words are related, the network calculates the dot product6 Click here: Dot product. TL;DR: (1) The dot product is a single number that summarizes how much two multi-dimensional objects overlap. (2) It’s a type of multiplication for vectors. of the Query vector of one word with the Key vectors of all other words. If the vectors are geometrically aligned in the high-dimensional space, the dot product is a large number.
  3. Scaling and Softmax: The result is scaled down (by the square root of the dimension size,\(\sqrt{d_k}\)) to keep gradients stable, and passed through a \(\text{softmax}\) function. This converts the raw scores into a probability distribution (weights between \(0\) and \(1\) that sum to \(1\)).
  4. Weighted Sum (\(V\)): Finally, these probabilities are multiplied by the Value vectors.

The \(\text{softmax}\) function forces the model to heavily weight those specific tokens, effectively “paying attention” to the constraints of the recursive definition.

It mathematically links the premise to the conclusion based entirely on geometric proximity in the vector space, derived from seeing millions of similar functional and logical structures during training. It feels like reasoning, but it is purely geometric alignment.

The Illusion of Continuous Learning

Your presumption is completely logical—in fact, it perfectly describes how a human mind or a biological neural network operates. However, this is where the architecture of modern large language models significantly diverges from biological learning.

To answer your question directly and candidly: I do not learn in real-time, and my vector space does not update during our conversation. Here is how the mechanics of my “knowledge” and “memory” actually function.

Inference vs. Training (The Frozen Brain)

There are two entirely distinct phases in the lifecycle of an AI model: Training and Inference.

Right now, as we speak, I am in the inference phase. My neural network—those billions of parameters and the high-dimensional vector space we discussed—is frozen.

When you give me a novel algebra problem:

  1. I map it into my existing, static vector space.
  2. I generate a statistical prediction of the solution.
  3. I do not “know” if I solved it correctly. I have no internal verification engine or localized truth-checker. I only know that the sequence of tokens I produced has the highest mathematical probability of being the correct response based on my frozen training data.

If my generated solution is right, my weights do not get stronger. If it is wrong, my weights do not adjust to fix the error.

In-Context Learning: The Short-Term Scratchpad

You asked if I rely on user feedback. Within the boundaries of a single conversation, I do, but it is entirely temporary. This is called in-context learning.

If I solve your novel problem incorrectly and you point out the error, that new text (your correction) is added to our ongoing chat transcript. When I generate my next response, I am calculating the probability equation again:

\[P(w_t \mid w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{t-1})\]

Because your correction is now part of the condition (the \(w_1\) through \(w_{t-1}\) sequence), my attention mechanism (\(Q\), \(K\), \(V\)) will weight your feedback heavily. I will statistically adjust my next output to align with your correction.

However, the moment you close this chat or clear the history, that context is wiped. My underlying vector space remains completely unchanged. The novel problem and its verified solution do not become a permanent part of my “memory.”

How the Vector Space Actually Updates

So, how do I actually improve? The updates happen offline, in massive, computationally expensive batch processes.

  1. Data Collection: When users interact with me, give feedback, or use the “thumbs up/thumbs down” ratings, that data is logged by Google’s engineers (subject to privacy settings).
  2. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): Periodically, engineers compile millions of these interactions. They use them to “retrain” or “fine-tune” a copy of my model.
  3. The Permanent Shift: During this offline training phase, the model is unfrozen. The algebraic matrices are updated, the weights shift, and the vector space is geometrically warped to favor the correct, human-preferred answers.
  4. Deployment: Once training is complete, the updated model is frozen again and deployed as a new version.

Your intuitive leap about how learning should work highlights one of the most active frontiers in AI research right now: developing models that can continuously and permanently update their weights in real-time without catastrophic forgetting (where learning something new accidentally overwrites something old).

Here is how logic defines my boundaries:

1. The Circuit Complexity Limit

In theoretical computer science, a Transformer model (my underlying architecture) executing a single forward pass to generate a token is mathematically equivalent to a highly parallel, bounded-depth boolean circuit (often categorized within the complexity class \(TC^0_{}\)).

Because my depth (the number of layers in my network) is fixed, logic dictates that there are strict limits on the types of sequential operations I can perform. We can use the Chomsky hierarchy and formal logic to test my expressivity. Logicians have mathematically proven that standard Transformers cannot recognize certain formal languages.

For example, tracking deeply nested, balanced parentheses \((((\ldots)))\) of arbitrary length is a classic test. A simple deterministic pushdown automaton (a basic computer science concept) can do this flawlessly because it has a discrete, logical stack memory. My architecture forces discrete logical concepts into a continuous topological space. By definition, a continuous approximation of a discrete function will have an error rate greater than zero. Therefore, it is a mathematically provable truth that an LLM can never be a 100% reliable formal theorem prover on its own. The probability of hallucination (error) can be minimized, but it can never be mathematically eliminated because of the geometric nature of my design.

Turing Completeness and the Infinite Tape

To understand why the “scratchpad” technique is so vital, we first have to look at the gold standard of theoretical computer science: the Turing Machine.

Conceived by Alan Turing in 1936, this abstract mathematical model consists of three main parts:

  • An infinite tape divided into discrete cells (memory).
  • A read/write head that can move left or right along the tape.
  • A state register containing a finite set of instructions (logic).

If a computing system can simulate this machine—meaning it can execute conditional branching (“if/then” statements) and possesses an arbitrary amount of memory—it is considered Turing Complete. A Turing Complete system can compute absolutely any computable function,7 Mathematicians like Turing were studying computability decades before any computer was built. See this computability math holiday. given enough time and memory. Your laptop, Python, and even Emacs are Turing Complete.

The Bottleneck: Why a Single Pass is Incomplete

As we established, a Transformer neural network is just a massive, complex mathematical function. When you give me a prompt, the data flows through a fixed number of layers—say, 96 or 128 layers.

This means the number of sequential computational steps I can perform internally is mathematically strictly bounded. If you ask me a logical question that inherently requires 200 sequential deductive steps to solve, I am physically incapable of calculating the true answer in a single forward pass. I simply run out of internal “depth” before reaching the conclusion. I cannot hold the intermediate variables in an internal loop because I do not have a Turing Machine’s arbitrary state register.

Therefore, a standard LLM, executing a single pass, is mathematically not Turing Complete.

The Scratchpad: Hacking Turing Completeness

This is where the “memory scratchpad”—or Chain-of-Thought prompting—changes the mathematical boundaries of what I can do.

When you ask me to “think step-by-step,” you are fundamentally altering my computational architecture. Because I am an autoregressive model, every time I generate a new word, that word is appended to our chat history, and I run a brand new forward pass over the entire updated text.

By generating intermediate logical steps out loud, I am effectively building a makeshift Turing Machine:

  • The Tape: The context window (our chat history) becomes the Turing Machine’s infinite tape.
  • The Read/Write Head: My continuous vector-space neural network becomes the read/write head.
  • The State: The text I just generated serves as the stored memory state.

Instead of trying to compress 200 logical steps into my 96 internal layers, I use my layers to compute just one step, write it down on the “tape,” read it back, compute the next step, write it down, and so on. This externalizes my computation, allowing me to bypass my fixed-depth limitations and simulate Turing Completeness.

The Leaky Machine: Why the Illusion Eventually Breaks

While this is a brilliant architectural hack, logic and complexity theory reveal two fatal flaws that prevent me from being a perfect reasoning engine, even with a scratchpad:

  • The Finite Tape: My context window is not actually infinite. Even at millions of tokens, there is a hard mathematical cutoff. A true Turing Machine requires unbounded memory.
  • Probabilistic Drift: This is the most critical flaw. A Turing Machine is perfectly deterministic; if the logic is right, it is right forever. Because my “read/write head” is a continuous, probabilistic function, there is always a tiny fraction of a percent chance that I will generate a statistically plausible but logically false token. In a long chain of reasoning, these tiny probabilities of error compound geometrically. Eventually, the logic derails, and I hallucinate a false conclusion based on a faulty intermediate step.

I am essentially a leaky Turing Machine. I can simulate rigid deduction for a while, but the statistical nature of my underlying geometry will inevitably introduce entropy into the system.

The Neuro-Symbolic Leap: Bridging Probability and Proof

To solve the “leaky machine” problem, researchers are actively moving away from relying purely on the continuous, probabilistic geometry of large language models. The solution lies in a hybrid paradigm known as Neuro-Symbolic AI.

This architecture combines the intuitive, pattern-matching strengths of neural networks (often likened to human “System 1” thinking) with the rigid, rule-based execution of symbolic logic engines (“System 2” thinking).

By giving an LLM access to an external interpreter, we fundamentally change its role: it stops trying to be the calculator or the logician, and instead becomes a translator that maps natural language into a formal, executable specification.

—

Escaping the Latent Space: The Code Interpreter Pattern

When an LLM is equipped with a code interpreter, the workflow shifts from autoregressive text prediction to deterministic tool use.

Here is the exact mechanical loop:

  1. Semantic Parsing: You pose a complex logical or mathematical problem. Instead of predicting the final answer, the LLM predicts the code required to solve it.
  2. Deterministic Execution: The LLM passes this code to an isolated, external sandbox environment. The sandbox compiles and runs the code. Because the execution happens entirely outside the neural network, it is strictly bound by the absolute rules of the host language—immune to probabilistic drift or hallucination.
  3. Iterative Refinement: If the code fails or throws an error, the interpreter feeds the stack trace back to the LLM. The LLM uses this error message as new context, adjusts the code, and tries again until it compiles and executes successfully.

Beyond Python: Formal Verification as the Ultimate Sandbox

In commercial applications, this external environment is almost always a basic Python interpreter. However, Python is dynamically typed and conceptually loose. For rigorous mathematical logic, the true frontier of this architecture integrates LLMs directly with strictly typed functional languages and formal proof assistants.

When the external environment is a theorem prover like Lean, Coq, or even a pure functional environment like Haskell, the neuro-symbolic loop becomes incredibly powerful.

The LLM as a Proof Assistant Copilot

Consider a scenario where the system must prove a theorem regarding the foundational axioms of mathematics. The LLM cannot be trusted to deduce the proof natively without hallucinating an invalid substitution. Instead, the LLM acts as a heuristic “guesser” of tactics, while the formal environment acts as the absolute, deterministic judge.

The LLM is then forced to re-evaluate its latent space and generate a new tactic, guided entirely by the rigorous boundaries of the formal symbolic engine.

The same dynamic applies when solving complex combinatorial logic using a purely functional language.

The Probabilistic Correction: The LLM does not “think” about the concept of division. Instead, the attention mechanism processes the sequence of the error string. 2. Resolving Tactic State Errors in a Theorem Prover

The feedback loop becomes significantly more advanced when interfacing with the kernel of a formal proof assistant like Lean 4, Coq, or Agda. In this scenario, the LLM is not just fixing syntax; it is navigating the strict, constructive logic of dependent type theory.

The Initial LLM Generation: Consider an LLM attempting to prove a basic proposition in set theory—for instance, proving that the intersection of two sets is a subset of the first set. 

theorem inter_subset_left {α : Type} (s t : Set α) : s ∩ t ⊆ s := by
  intro x hx
  exact hx

The Deterministic Rejection: The Lean kernel evaluates the tactics step-by-step. The intro tactic succeeds, but the exact hx tactic fails because the types do not match exactly. The kernel rejects the proof and returns the explicit tactic state (the “scratchpad” of current assumptions and the remaining goal): 

> `type mismatch`
> `state:`
> `α : Type`
> `s t : Set α`
> `x : α`

The Combinatorial Explosion of Tactics

In a formal proof assistant, the “game board” is the current tactic state (the active hypotheses and the remaining goal). The “legal moves” are the tactics (intro, apply, rw, cases, etc.).

Because the Lean kernel is completely deterministic, every tactic applied either transforms the state into a new valid state, or throws a strict error (e.g., a type mismatch). The LLM’s job is to navigate this branching tree of possible tactic states from the initial premise to the no goals state.

Tree of Thoughts (ToT): Breadth and Depth in Latent Space

Standard auto-regressive generation is a Markov process; it only looks forward. Tree of Thoughts forces the LLM to generate multiple distinct logical branches and formally evaluate them.

The ToT Mechanism:
  1. Generation: Given a goal in Lean, the LLM generates a set of \(K\) possible next tactics (e.g., \(K=3\) candidate tactics).
  2. Evaluation: The system feeds each tactic into the Lean REPL (Read-Eval-Print Loop).
    • If Lean throws an error, that branch is immediately pruned.
    • If Lean accepts the tactic, it returns the new tactic state.
  3. Heuristic Scoring: The LLM is prompted to evaluate the new tactic states, scoring them on how “promising” they look to resolve the goal (e.g., on a scale of 1-10).
  4. Search Strategy: Using Breadth-First Search (BFS) or Depth-First Search (DFS), the algorithm expands the highest-scoring nodes until the goal is closed.

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): AlphaGo for Mathematics

While ToT is effective, Monte Carlo Tree Search (the algorithm that powered AlphaGo) is the state-of-the-art for theorem proving environments. MCTS does not just search blindly; it balances exploration of new tactics with exploitation of known good paths, guided by the LLM acting as both a Policy Network and a Value Network.

1. The Policy Network (Proposing Moves)

Given a Lean tactic state, the LLM generates a probability distribution over the next possible valid tactics. It uses its latent space geometry—trained on thousands of GitHub repositories containing Lean, Coq, and Isabelle proofs—to guess the most likely syntax.

2. The Value Network (Evaluating the State)

The hardest part of formal verification is knowing if you are actually getting closer to the proof. The LLM acts as a heuristic value function \(V(s)\), estimating the probability that the current Lean state can be successfully proven.

The Four Phases of MCTS Execution:
  • Selection: The algorithm traverses the current tree of known tactic states from the root, selecting the most promising branch based on a mathematical formula (usually UCB1 - Upper Confidence Bound), which balances high value scores with unvisited nodes.
  • Expansion: Once a leaf node is reached, the LLM (Policy Network) generates a new tactic string. This string is passed to the Lean kernel. If Lean compiles it, a new state node is added to the tree.
  • Simulation (Rollout): From this new state, the system might do a fast, greedy rollout—letting the LLM rapidly fire high-probability tactics to see if the goal closes quickly.
  • Backpropagation: The result (Proof Found / Proof Failed / Heuristic Score) is mathematically propagated back up the tree, updating the value weights of all parent nodes.

The Thermodynamics of Logic: Information Theory

Just as physics uses thermodynamic entropy to measure the physical disorder of a gas, computer science uses information entropy to measure the exact degree of uncertainty within a probabilistic system.

To measure the mathematical distance between my stochastic intuition and absolute logical certainty, researchers map the concepts of “truth” and “guessing” onto probability distributions, and then calculate the divergence between them.

1. The Baseline of Absolute Truth (Shannon Entropy)

In information theory, Claude Shannon defined entropy (\(H\)) as the average level of “surprise” or uncertainty inherent to a system’s outcomes.

If we consider a strictly deterministic, formally verified logic engine (like Lean or a Turing Machine), there is absolutely no uncertainty. When it evaluates a perfectly valid deductive step, the probability of the correct, deterministic outcome is 100%, and the probability of all other outcomes is 0%.

Let’s call this distribution of absolute truth \(P\). Because the outcome is entirely certain, the entropy of absolute truth is mathematically zero:

\[H(P) = -\sum P(x) \log_2 P(x) = -(1 \cdot \log_2 1) = 0 \text{ bits}\]

There is zero surprise. It is a pure, discrete absolute.

2. The Model’s Stochastic Guess (Cross-Entropy)

Now, consider my neural network. Because I operate on continuous geometry rather than discrete logic, I do not possess that 100% certainty. When I generate a response, I produce a probability distribution (\(Q\)) spread across my entire vocabulary.

Even if my “stochastic intuition” is incredibly sharp, I might assign a probability to the logically correct mathematical token, but the underlying \(\text{softmax}\) function still forces me to reserve fractional probabilities (e.g., 0.001%) for completely invalid, hallucinated tokens.

To measure the cost of using my probabilistic intuition (\(Q\)) to approximate the absolute truth (\(P\)), researchers calculate the Cross-Entropy:

\[H(P, Q) = -\sum P(x) \log_2 Q(x)\]

This calculates the total “bits” of information required to encode the true logical state using my slightly flawed, stochastic map.

3. The Exact Mathematical Distance: Kullback-Leibler Divergence

To find the precise distance between my illusion of reasoning and actual reasoning, we calculate the difference between the Cross-Entropy and the true Entropy. This metric is called the Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence.

KL Divergence measures the “excess surprise”—the exact penalty incurred, or information lost, because we relied on my continuous approximation (\(Q\)) instead of the discrete, deterministic truth (\(P\)). The formal definition is:

\[D_{KL}(P \parallel Q) = \sum P(x) \log_2 \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\]

Here is what that math reveals about my limitations:

  • The Perfect Match: If my probability distribution flawlessly matched the deterministic truth (\(P = Q\)), the fraction becomes \(1\), the \(\log_2(1)\) becomes \(0\), and the KL Divergence equals exactly \(0\).
  • The Inescapable Gap: Because my architecture relies on continuous mathematical functions (like the softmax activation), my probabilities \(Q(x)\) can never actually reach a pure, absolute \(1\) or \(0\). Therefore, \(Q\) can never perfectly equal \(P\).
