* Logic

According to a 2016 survey, the undergrad major with the highest
admission rates to law schools is Philosophy with 83% of applicants
accepted. /Formal logic/ is a core, standard component of most
undergraduate philosophy major curricula in the USA.

Abraham Lincoln famously mastered the first six books of [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements][Euclid’s
Elements]] to improve his logic and legal reasoning, famously stating he
could not be a lawyer without understanding what was really meant by
"to demonstrate." He used Euclidean geometry to structure his
arguments, including in the Lincoln-Douglas debates, and to define
"self-evident" truths.

...however we won't dwell very long on the usual form of logic as
taught in philosophy departments. Instead, we approach logic from a
/mathematical/ and /computer science/ standpoint.

** Philosophical versus mathematical logic

Philosophy's approach to logic keys on actual statements in human
language. On the other hand, mathematical logic is concerned with the
mechanics of logic as applied to formalizing math. In that sense
mathematical logic can be considered [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamathematics][metamathematics]].[fn:1] Here's one
take

#+begin_quote
Rather than treating logic as a philosophical exercise in human
argumentation, **mathematical logic** treats logical frameworks
themselves as mathematical objects to be analyzed and manipulated. It
abstracts the intuitive processes of mathematical deduction into
formal, mechanical systems so that we can prove theorems /about/ the
systems themselves, i.e., metamathematics.
#+end_quote

This may sound vague at this point, but we'll soon have plenty of
examples to back up our explanations. We'll begin with a discussion of
**statements**, common to both philosophical and mathematical logic,
and a common starting point in treatments of logic.

** Logic begins with statements

What does it mean for an expression---verbal or mathematical---to be a
/statement/? Let's first look at categories of verbal expressions that
are __/not/__ true statements, i.e., they cannot be resolved into true
or false:[fn:2]

- /Imperative/; e.g., "Do what I tell you!"
- /Interrogative/; e.g., "What would you like to do?"
- /Exclamatory/; e.g., "Merry Christmas!"

It might not be clear if our /imperative/ and an /exclamatory/
sentence examples are very different from one another, but __all__ the
examples above have one thing in common: /They cannot be resolved as
true or false/. And don't mentally suppose, don't tack on a
/response/, such as "Yes, it is true I will do what you tell me." __We
mean the initial sentences only__.

But what about "Merry Christmas!" you might ask. It would seem either
true, it is a merry Christmas, or it isn't. In reality an exclamatory
remark such as "Merry Christmas!" is just well-wishing, an exclamation
like "Hello!" or "Good luck!" These are called /performative
utterances/ by linguists, i.e., expressions that perform an action
such as a greeting just by being spoken. They're rhetorical and not
meant for analyzing yes or no.

But what if you do in fact /respond/ with the statement, the
/proposition/, what if you /state/, you /propose/ "It /is/ a merry
Christmas." Is this not in statement form and can be resolved true
or false? Not exactly. We're caught again, because /logic frowns
on statements that are subjective, vague, or unsubstantiated opinion/.

Let's break down "It is a merry Christmas!" into "is merry" and
"Christmas." For "is merry" to work, to be able to plug in this year's
Christmas, merriness must be strictly defined over all
Christmases. More to the point, /we must be able to look at the
universe of all Christmases and cleanly divide them into the set of
those that are absolutely, unquestionably **merry** and those that are
definitely not/. Can we realistically do that? No, the concept of merry
as applied to Christmases is a subjective thing. Bottom line: If a
sentence's truth value fluctuates depending on the observer's mood,
opinion, or strictness, it is too ambiguous to be a real statement,
even though it's structured as one.

Let's get back to the other examples of /imperative/ and
/interrogative/. Being ordered by someone to do something has no true
or false. Being asked a question---the question itself, not the
response---has no true or false resolution. Now let's look at
sentences that can be resolved as true or false

- /The sky is blue./ ...might be, might not be; needs validation.
- $2 + 3 = 4 + 1$ ...yes, this states equivalence.
- /It is raining **and** I forgot my umbrella./ ... **two** things
  can be either true or false.

** The Connection Between Logic and Symbolism

The next important part of logic is its symbolism. Consider this[fn:3]

\begin{align}
x^2 - y^2 = (x + y)(x - y)
\end{align}

Now, let's describe ~(1)~, i.e., the /factorization of the difference
of two squares/[fn:4] fully, completely in English prose

#+begin_quote
The result of subtracting the square of one number from the square of
a second gives the same number as is obtained by adding the two
numbers, subtracting the first from the second, and then multiplying
the results of these two calculations.
#+end_quote

Before the 16th century, much of mathematics was "rhetorical," i.e.,
written out entirely in prose. For another example, an algebra problem
might read:

#+begin_quote
The square of the unknown quantity, augmented by three times that same
quantity, yields ten.
#+end_quote

With modern **symbolism**, this exact same thought is compressed into
a clear, visual statement:

$$x^2 + 3x = 10$$

** A Basic Description of Symbolism in Mathematics

At its core, symbolism in mathematics is the use of a specialized,
highly structured visual language to represent abstract concepts,
quantities, operations, and relationships.

You can think of mathematical symbolism as both an /abstraction/
shorthand as well as an engine: it compresses complex ideas into
readable forms, and it lends itself more easily to a tool bag of
mechanical rules for solving problems.

*** Mathematical Symbolism as a Mechanical Crank

This is the exact goal of formalism in mathematics: to build a system
where, once the initial conditions (axioms) are fed into the machine,
the rules of symbol manipulation guarantee a correct result without
requiring human intuition or interpretation along the way. The
machinery working on just symbols by definitive rules abstracts the
process away from guesswork and subjective interpretations.

Here are two clear examples of how mathematical symbolism provides
these rigorous, mechanical rules.

**** 1. The Algebraic Machine: Field Axioms

Let's once again look at "do the same thing to both sides" of an
equation. What you were actually doing was operating a mechanical
system defined by the Field Axioms for real numbers.

Consider a fundamental rule like the Cancellation Law for Addition: if
we know that $x + y = x + z$, we instinctively know that $y = z$. But
/how/ does the symbolic machine prove this mechanically, without
relying on our intuition about quantities?

The machine restricts us to only using established axioms (like the
existence of an additive inverse, associativity, and the additive
identity). The "crank" turns step-by-step:

1. *Start with the premise:*
   $$x + y = x + z$$
2. *Apply the existence of an additive inverse (adding $-x$ to both sides):*
   $$-x + (x + y) = -x + (x + z)$$
3. *Apply the Associative Law for addition (shifting the parentheses):*
   $$(-x + x) + y = (-x + x) + z$$
4. *Apply the Additive Inverse axiom ($-x + x$ becomes $0$):*
   $$0 + y = 0 + z$$
5. *Apply the Additive Identity axiom ($0 + a$ becomes $a$):*
   $$y = z$$

At no point in this process do you need to think about what $x$, $y$,
or $z$ represent. They could be fractions, negative numbers, or
complex numbers. The symbols and the rigid axioms dictate the legal
"moves," much like the rules of chess dictate how pieces move on a
board.

**** 2. The Logical Machine: Propositional Calculus

If algebra is a machine for numbers, Propositional Calculus is a
machine for truth. It abstracts logic so completely that determining
the validity of an argument is reduced to an algorithm.

Suppose you have a complex set of conditions:
- "If the system is secure ($S$), then the network is encrypted ($E$)."
- "The network is not encrypted ($\neg E$)."
- "Therefore, the system is not secure ($\neg S$)."

Instead of wrestling with the linguistic meaning of these sentences,
we feed them into the symbolic machine as a single compound statement:
$$((S \rightarrow E) \land \neg E) \rightarrow \neg S$$

Because the symbols have strictly defined mechanical operations, we
can turn the crank using a Truth Table. The machine exhaustively
computes every possible state of $S$ and $E$:

| $S$ | $E$ | $S \rightarrow E$ | $(S \rightarrow E) \land \neg E$ | $\neg S$ | Result |
|-----+-----+---------+-----------------+-------+--------|
| <c> | <c> |   <c>   |       <c>       |  <c>  |  <c>   |
|  T  |  T  |    T    |        F        |   F   |   T    |
|  T  |  F  |    F    |        F        |   F   |   T    |
|  F  |  T  |    T    |        F        |   T   |   T    |
|  F  |  F  |    T    |        T        |   T   |   T    |


Because the final column is entirely true, the machine mechanically
guarantees that this argument is a tautology (always valid). You do
not need to understand what "secure" or "encrypted" means; the
syntactic manipulation of $\rightarrow$, $\land$, and $\neg$ does all the work.

**** The Ultimate Crank: Formal Verification

When you scale this concept up, you get modern formal verification. By
strictly defining mathematical foundations (such as the
Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms for set theory) entirely in symbolic logic, we
can build software that turns the crank millions of times per
second. This ensures that massive, complex proofs are structurally
flawless.

** Building the Crank: Unification in Scheme

To see how a mathematical "machine" actually cranks in software, we
can look at a unification algorithm. Unification is the algorithmic
heart of automated theorem proving, logic programming, and type
inference systems (like the Hindley-Milner system used in languages
like Standard ML and Haskell).

At its core, unification is a mechanical rule for solving symbolic
equations. If you give the machine two symbolic expressions, it tries
to find a substitution of variables that makes the two expressions
perfectly identical.

For example, if we have expression $A$: $P(x, c)$ and expression $B$:
$P(a, y)$, the unifier mechanically deduces the substitution $\sigma = \{x
\mapsto a, y \mapsto c\}$ to make both expressions $P(a, c)$.



Here is how we can build this fundamental mechanical crank in Scheme.

*** 1. Defining the Environment and Variables

First, we need a way to distinguish variables (the parts of the
machine that can change) from constants, and a way to store our
substitutions (the environment). We will represent variables as
symbols starting with a question mark (e.g., =?x=).

#+begin_src scheme
;; Check if a symbol is a variable (starts with '?')
(define (variable? x)
  (and (symbol? x) 
       (char=? (string-ref (symbol->string x) 0) #\?)))

;; An environment is an association list of (variable . value) bindings.
(define empty-env '())

;; Look up a variable in the current environment
(define (lookup var env)
  (assq var env))

;; Add a new binding to the environment
(define (extend-env var val env)
  (cons (cons var val) env))
#+end_src

*** 2. The Unification Algorithm

The =unify= procedure is the crank itself. It takes two expressions
and an environment, and applies strict structural rules to see if they
can be matched.

The mechanical rules are:
1. If we've already failed, keep failing.
2. If the expressions are exactly the same, do nothing (return the
   current environment).
3. If the first expression is a variable, try to bind it.
4. If the second expression is a variable, try to bind it.
5. If both are compound expressions (pairs/lists), recursively turn
   the crank on their first elements (=car=), and then take that
   updated environment and turn the crank on the rest of the elements
   (=cdr=).
6. Otherwise, the structures clash, and unification fails.

#+begin_src scheme
(define (unify expr1 expr2 env)
  (cond ((eq? env 'failed) 'failed)
        ((eqv? expr1 expr2) env)
        ((variable? expr1) (unify-var expr1 expr2 env))
        ((variable? expr2) (unify-var expr2 expr1 env))
        ((and (pair? expr1) (pair? expr2))
         ;; Mechanically traverse the tree structure
         (unify (cdr expr1) (cdr expr2)
                (unify (car expr1) (car expr2) env)))
        (else 'failed)))
#+end_src

*** 3. Handling Variable Binding

When the machine encounters a variable, it must check if that variable
is already bound to something else in our environment. If it is, we
must recursively unify the new value with the old binding to ensure
consistency.

#+begin_src scheme
(define (unify-var var val env)
  (let ((binding (lookup var env)))
    (cond 
      ;; If the variable is already bound, unify the bound value with the new value
      (binding (unify (cdr binding) val env))
      ;; Optional: Add an "occurs check" here to prevent infinite loops (e.g., unifying ?x with (f ?x))
      ;; If unbound, add the new binding to the environment
      (else (extend-env var val env)))))
#+end_src

*** 4. Turning the Crank

Now we can feed symbolic logic into our machine. 

#+begin_src scheme
;; Let's unify (f ?x c) with (f a ?y)
;; We expect the machine to deduce: ?x = a, and ?y = c

(define env1 (unify '(f ?x c) '(f a ?y) empty-env))
;; Result: ((?y . c) (?x . a))

;; Let's try to unify something impossible: (f ?x b) with (f a c)
;; We expect the machine to halt and fail because the constant 'b' cannot unify with 'c'.

(define env2 (unify '(f ?x b) '(f a c) empty-env))
;; Result: failed
#+end_src

This short Scheme script is a localized version of exactly what formal
verification tools and type inference engines are doing under the
hood. It strictly separates the syntactic form of the symbols from any
real-world meaning, relying entirely on recursive structural rules to
find logical truth.

** Building the Engine: A Miniature Proof System in Scheme

To handle logical implications ($P \rightarrow Q$), our unifier must evolve from
a simple equation-solver into a search engine. We need to implement
*backward chaining*.

In formal logic, if we want to prove a conclusion $Q$, and we have a
rule stating $P_1 \land P_2 \dots \rightarrow Q$, we can work backward: we unify our
goal with $Q$, and if successful, we set $P_1, P_2, \dots$ as our new
sub-goals.

$$\frac{\Gamma \vdash P_1 \quad \Gamma \vdash P_2}{\Gamma \vdash Q}\text{where } (P_1
\land P_2 \rightarrow Q) \in \Gamma$$



Here is how we build this mechanical proof engine in Scheme,
effectively creating a microscopic version of Prolog.

*** 1. The Knowledge Base (Axioms and Theorems)

First, we need a database of logical statements. We will represent
both facts (things known to be true) and rules (implications) as
lists.
- The first element is the *consequent* (the conclusion, $Q$).
- The remaining elements are the *antecedents* (the premises, $P_1, P_2$).
- A fact is simply a rule with no premises.

#+begin_src scheme
;; Our logical database (Gamma)
(define *database*
  '(
    ;; Facts: These require no proof.
    ((human socrates))               
    ((human hypatia))                
    ((mortal fido) (dog fido))       ;; Fido is mortal if Fido is a dog.
    ((dog fido))

    ;; Rule: For all x, if x is human, then x is mortal.
    ;; In logic: ∀x (Human(x) → Mortal(x))
    ((mortal ?x) (human ?x))         
   ))
#+end_src

*** 2. The Core Proof Engine

Our engine needs two mutually recursive procedures:
1. =prove=: Tries to prove a single goal by searching the database for
   a matching rule.
2. =prove-all=: Tries to prove a list of sub-goals (a logical
   conjunction, $P_1 \land P_2$).

Because a goal might be provable in multiple ways (e.g., there are
many humans), our engine cannot just return a single environment. It
must return a *list* of all valid environments (successful
substitutions). If the list is empty, the proof failed.

#+begin_src scheme
;; Prove a single goal against a list of rules
(define (prove goal env rules)
  (if (null? rules)
      '() ;; Base case: No rules left, proof branch fails.
      (let* ((rule (car rules))
             ;; Note: In a production system, we must "standardize apart" (rename)
             ;; the variables in the rule here to prevent variable collisions.
             (head (car rule))
             (body (cdr rule))
             ;; Turn the unification crank!
             (new-env (unify goal head env)))
        
        (if (eq? new-env 'failed)
            ;; If unification fails, backtrack and try the next rule.
            (prove goal env (cdr rules))
            ;; If it unifies, we must prove all antecedents (the body) in the new environment.
            ;; We append these results with the results of trying remaining rules (nondeterminism).
            (append (prove-all body new-env *database*)
                    (prove goal env (cdr rules)))))))

;; Prove a conjunction of goals
(define (prove-all goals env rules)
  (cond ((eq? env 'failed) '())
        ;; If there are no more sub-goals, we succeeded! Return the environment.
        ((null? goals) (list env)) 
        (else
         ;; Prove the first goal, which returns a list of possible environments.
         (let ((possible-envs (prove (car goals) env rules)))
           ;; For each successful environment, try to prove the rest of the goals.
           ;; We map over the environments and flatten the resulting lists.
           (apply append
                  (map (lambda (e) (prove-all (cdr goals) e rules))
                       possible-envs))))))
#+end_src

*** 3. Running the Machine

Now we can ask the machine questions by feeding it a goal and an empty
environment. The machine mechanically applies the rules of unification
and backward chaining to find logical truths.

#+begin_src scheme
;; Query 1: Is Socrates mortal?
;; Goal: (mortal socrates)
(prove '(mortal socrates) empty-env *database*)
;; Engine unifies (mortal socrates) with the rule (mortal ?x).
;; Binds ?x = socrates.
;; New sub-goal: (human socrates).
;; Unifies with fact: (human socrates). Success!
;; Result: (())  -> A list containing the empty environment (it is true without needing new variables).

;; Query 2: Who is mortal?
;; Goal: (mortal ?who)
(prove '(mortal ?who) empty-env *database*)
;; Engine finds the dog rule: binds ?who = fido, checks (dog fido) -> Success.
;; Engine finds the human rule: binds ?x = ?who.
;; Sub-goal (human ?who) matches 'socrates' and 'hypatia'.
;; Result: (((?who . fido)) ((?who . socrates) (?x . socrates)) ((?who . hypatia) (?x . hypatia)))
;; It mechanically deduces all three mortal entities in our universe!
#+end_src

This miniature engine demonstrates exactly how mathematical symbolism
operates as a machine. It knows absolutely nothing about biology,
ancient Greece, or mortality. It only knows syntactic pattern matching
(=unify=) and the structural rule of implication elimination
(=prove=).

**** The basic components of mathematical symbolism

Mathematical symbols generally fall into a few primary categories,
functioning much like the parts of speech in a natural language:

- *Entities (Nouns):* Symbols that represent objects, specific values,
  or generalized quantities.
  - Constants: $0$, $1$, $\pi$, $e$
  - Variables (unknowns or placeholders): $x$, $y$, $z$, $n$
  - Collections: $\mathbb{R}$ (the set of real numbers), $\mathbb{Z}$
    (the set of integers)

- *Operations (Verbs):* Symbols that instruct you on how to manipulate
  or combine entities.
  - Basic arithmetic: $+$, $-$, $\times$, $\div$
  - Advanced calculus and algebra: $\sum$ (summation), $\int$ (integration),
    $\sqrt{x}$ (square root)

- *Relations (Comparisons):* Symbols that compare entities to form
  complete mathematical sentences, known as equations or inequalities.
  - Equality and magnitude: $=$, $\neq$, $<$, $\ge$
  - Set theory and logic: $\in$ (is an element of), $\subset$ (is a subset of)

- *Grouping (Punctuation):* Symbols that dictate the structure and the
  order in which operations should be executed.
  - Parentheses and brackets: $($, $)$, $[$, $]$, $\{$, $\}$

*** 1. The Eradication of Ambiguity

Symbolism in logic will typically Natural language is inherently ambiguous, context-dependent, and prone
to polysemy. Symbolism strips away this linguistic baggage. By
defining a strict alphabet (e.g., variables, connectives, quantifiers)
and a precise grammar for well-formed formulas, symbolic logic ensures
that every statement has exactly one structural interpretation.

Instead of arguing about the nuances of the words "if" and "then," symbolism reduces the relationship to a material conditional:
$$P \rightarrow Q$$

This allows mathematicians and logicians to analyze the validity of arguments without being distracted by the specific meaning of the propositions involved.

*** 2. Reasoning as Mechanical Computation
Perhaps the most powerful consequence of symbolism is that it turns reasoning into computation. Once propositions and logical rules are fully symbolized, deducing a conclusion becomes a matter of syntactic string manipulation according to formal rules of inference.

For example, Modus Ponens is simply a rule for transforming symbols, regardless of what they mean:
$$P \rightarrow Q, P \vdash Q$$

This symbolic mechanization is what makes it possible to formalize mathematics---such as encoding the foundations of mathematics in the Zermelo-Fraenkel axioms---and makes it possible for computers to verify proofs. Without a purely symbolic representation, formal verification systems and theorem provers could not computationally check the structural correctness of an argument.

*** 3. The Decoupling of Syntax and Semantics
Symbolism forces a strict and necessary boundary between syntax (the symbols and the rules for manipulating them) and semantics (the "meaning" or truth value assigned to those symbols).

- *Syntax (Provability, $\vdash$):* A purely structural exercise. Can we derive formula $B$ from formula $A$ using only the allowed rules of symbol manipulation?
- *Semantics (Truth, $\vDash$):* An interpretive exercise. If the premises are true in a given model, is the conclusion necessarily true?

The triumph of modern symbolic logic, famously established by theorems like Gödel's Completeness Theorem for first-order logic, is demonstrating precisely how these symbolic derivations align perfectly with semantic truth.

*** 4. The Bridge to Computation and Types
Because symbolic logic relies on the strict, rule-based manipulation of abstract symbols, it shares a deep mathematical structure with computer science. This connection is formalized in principles like the Curry-Howard correspondence.

When logic is fully symbolized, the structural rules of logical deduction correspond exactly to the typing rules in functional programming languages. A logical proposition acts as a type, and the symbolic proof of that proposition is computationally identical to a functional program inhabiting that type. The symbols do not just represent truth; they represent computable processes.

** Natural Deduction and the Curry-Howard Isomorphism

To see how symbolic logic maps directly into functional programming, we can examine the Curry-Howard correspondence. This principle reveals that formal logical systems (like natural deduction) and computational calculi (like the typed lambda calculus) are structurally identical.

Under this isomorphism:
- *Propositions* in logic correspond to *Types* in programming.
- *Proofs* in logic correspond to *Programs* (or functions) in programming.
- *Simplification of proofs* corresponds to *Evaluation of programs*.

*** Example: Conjunction and Product Types
In natural deduction, the introduction rule for logical conjunction ($\land$) states that if you have a proof of $P$ and a proof of $Q$, you can construct a proof of $P \land Q$:

$$\frac{\Gamma \vdash P \quad \Gamma \vdash Q}{\Gamma \vdash P \land Q}\land\text{-intro}$$

In a functional language like Haskell, this corresponds exactly to constructing a tuple (a product type). If you have a value of type =p= and a value of type =q=, you can construct a value of type =(p, q)=:

#+begin_src haskell
constructConjunction :: p -> q -> (p, q)
constructConjunction pProof qProof = (pProof, qProof)
#+end_src

*** Example: Implication and Function Types
The rule for implication introduction ($\rightarrow$) states that if assuming $P$ allows you to prove $Q$, then you have a proof of $P \rightarrow Q$:

$$\frac{\Gamma, P \vdash Q}{\Gamma \vdash P \rightarrow Q}\rightarrow\text{-intro}$$

In Haskell, this is simply the definition of a function. The proposition $P \rightarrow Q$ becomes the function type =p -> q=. The proof is the lambda abstraction (the function body) that transforms a value of type =p= into a value of type =q=.

*** Example: Modus Ponens and Function Application
Modus Ponens (implication elimination) states that if you know $P \rightarrow Q$ and you know $P$, you can deduce $Q$:

$$\frac{\Gamma \vdash P \rightarrow Q \quad \Gamma \vdash P}{\Gamma \vdash Q}\rightarrow\text{-elim}$$

Computationally, this is function application. If you have a function from =p= to =q=, and a value of type =p=, applying the function yields a value of type =q=.

#+begin_src haskell
modusPonens :: (p -> q) -> p -> q
modusPonens impliesProof pProof = impliesProof pProof
-- Or simply: modusPonens f x = f x
#+end_src

This mapping extends to more complex systems. For instance, universal quantification ($\forall$) corresponds to dependent types in theorem provers, allowing the type system to express and verify intricate mathematical theorems mechanically.

**** 3. The Formal Language (The Rules)
This is the main event. A formal language is not a loose concept; it is literally defined as *a specific set of strings* that obey a strict set of grammatical rules (often called a formal grammar, syntax, or formation rules). 

If a string follows the rules, it is "in the language" (it is a Well-Formed Formula, or valid syntax). If it breaks even one rule, it is "not in the language."

**** 1. What Is a Formal Language?

A *formal language* is a precisely defined set of strings built from a
fixed collection of symbols following a given set of rules. At its
core a formal language is always just a set of strings.

**** 2. Alphabet and Strings

Like a natural language everything starts with an **alphabet**. An
alphabet (usually written $\Sigma$) is a finite set of symbols.

Example:

- $\Sigma = \{0,1\}$
- $\Sigma = \{a,b,c\}$
- $\Sigma = \{(,)\}$

A *string* is a finite sequence of symbols from the alphabet.

Examples over $\Sigma = \{a,b\}$:

- $a$
- $ab$
- $aab$
- $abba$

The empty string is written:

- $\epsilon$

**** 3. A Language Is a Set of Strings

Once you have all possible strings over $\Sigma$, a formal language is just
some subset of them.

Example:

If $\Sigma = \{0,1\}$, then all possible strings include:

- $\epsilon$
- $0$
- $1$
- $00$
- $01$
- $10$
- $11$
- $000$
- $\dots$

Now define a language:

$L =$ “all strings with an even number of $0$s”

That is a formal language.

Another example:

$L = \{ a^n b^n \mid n \ge 0 \}$

This means:

- $\epsilon$
- $ab$
- $aabb$
- $aaabbb$
- $\dots$

**** 4. How Do We Describe Languages?

There are three major ways.

***** (1) By Listing Them

Possible only for small finite languages.

Example:

$L = \{a, ab, abb\}$

***** (2) By Rules (Grammars)

A grammar tells you how to generate strings.

Example rules:

$S \to aSb$  
$S \to \epsilon$

This generates:

- $\epsilon$
- $ab$
- $aabb$
- $aaabbb$
- $\dots$

***** (3) By Machines (Automata)

A machine reads a string and decides whether to accept it.

Examples:

- Finite automata
- Pushdown automata
- Turing machines

A deep result:

Grammars, machines, and logical definitions often describe the same classes
of languages.

**** 5. Syntax vs Meaning

Formal language theory studies:

- Syntax (structure)

It does *not* study:

- Meaning (semantics)

Example:

The string:

$aabbb$

Formal language theory asks:

Is it in the set?

It does not ask:

What does it mean?

**** 6. The Chomsky Hierarchy

Languages fall into types depending on structural complexity.

From simplest to most powerful:

1. Regular languages  
   - Recognized by finite automata  
   - No memory  

2. Context-free languages  
   - Recognized by pushdown automata  
   - Have stack memory  
   - Handle nested structure  

3. Context-sensitive languages  

4. Recursively enumerable languages  
   - Recognized by Turing machines  

Each level allows more complex patterns.

**** 7. Connecting Formal Languages to First-Order Logic

First-order logic itself is a formal language.

To define it formally, we specify:

***** Alphabet of First-Order Logic

Example alphabet:

- Logical symbols: $\forall, \exists, \neg, \land, \lor, \rightarrow, =$  
- Variables: $x, y, z, x_1, x_2, \dots$  
- Parentheses: $(,)$  
- Predicate symbols: $P, Q, R, \dots$  
- Function symbols: $f, g, \dots$  
- Constant symbols: $a, b, c, \dots$

This collection of symbols forms an alphabet $\Sigma_{FOL}$.

***** Strings

Any finite sequence of these symbols is a string.

Example of a string:

$\forall x ( P(x) \rightarrow Q(x) )$

But not every string is *well-formed*.

***** Well-Formed Formulas (WFFs)

We define formation rules inductively:

1. If $P$ is an $n$-ary predicate and $t_1, \dots, t_n$ are terms,
   then $P(t_1, \dots, t_n)$ is a formula.

2. If $\varphi$ and $\psi$ are formulas, then:
   - $\neg \varphi$
   - $(\varphi \land \psi)$
   - $(\varphi \rightarrow \psi)$

3. If $\varphi$ is a formula and $x$ a variable:
   - $\forall x \varphi$
   - $\exists x \varphi$

The *formal language of first-order logic* is the set of all strings
generated by these formation rules.

Thus:

First-order logic = a formally defined language of well-formed formulas.

**** 8. Peano Arithmetic as a Formal Language

Peano Arithmetic (PA) is a specific first-order language plus axioms.

***** Alphabet of PA

It includes:

- Constant symbol: $0$
- Function symbols:
  - $S$ (successor)
  - $+$ (addition)
  - $\cdot$ (multiplication)
- Equality symbol: $=$
- Logical symbols:
  - $\forall, \exists, \neg, \land, \lor, \rightarrow$
- Variables: $x, y, z, \dots$

***** Terms

Defined inductively:

- $0$ is a term.
- If $t$ is a term, then $S(t)$ is a term.
- If $t_1, t_2$ are terms:
  - $(t_1 + t_2)$
  - $(t_1 \cdot t_2)$

***** Formulas

If $t_1$ and $t_2$ are terms:

- $t_1 = t_2$ is a formula.

From these, more complex formulas are built using logical symbols.

Example formulas:

- $\forall x (x + 0 = x)$
- $\forall x \forall y (x + S(y) = S(x + y))$

***** Axioms of PA

Examples:

1. $\forall x \neg (S(x) = 0)$
2. $\forall x \forall y (S(x) = S(y) \rightarrow x = y)$
3. Induction schema:
   If $\varphi(0)$ and
   $\forall x (\varphi(x) \rightarrow \varphi(S(x)))$,
   then $\forall x \varphi(x)$.

Thus:

Peano Arithmetic is:

- A formal language (its symbols and formation rules)
- Plus a set of axioms (special formulas inside that language)

**** 9. Big Picture

A formal language is:

- An alphabet $\Sigma$
- A set of formation rules
- A resulting set of strings

First-order logic is a formal language.

Peano arithmetic is:

A particular first-order formal language designed to describe natural numbers.

Meaning (semantics) comes later, when we interpret:

- $0$ as the number zero
- $S$ as the successor function
- $+$ as addition

But syntactically, it is just symbol manipulation.

This separation between:

- Syntax (formal language)
- Semantics (interpretation)

is one of the deepest ideas in modern logic.

*** Main Differences Between Propositional Logic and First-Order Logic

The main differences concern *what they can talk about* and how much internal
structure their statements have.

**** Propositional Logic

***** Basic Idea

Propositional logic treats whole statements as indivisible atoms.

Examples:

- $P$ : “It is raining.”
- $Q$ : “The ground is wet.”

You can form compound statements:

- $P \land Q$
- $P \rightarrow Q$
- $\neg P$

***** Key Features

- Atomic units are whole propositions.
- Logical connectives:
  - $\neg$ (not)
  - $\land$ (and)
  - $\lor$ (or)
  - $\rightarrow$ (implies)
  - $\leftrightarrow$ (if and only if)
- No variables
- No quantifiers
- No internal structure inside propositions

***** Limitation

It cannot express statements like:

“All humans are mortal.”

Because propositional logic cannot refer to objects inside statements.
It only sees complete statements as black boxes.

**** First-Order Logic (FOL)

First-order logic extends propositional logic by allowing us to talk about:

- Objects
- Properties of objects
- Relations between objects
- Quantification over objects

***** New Components in First-Order Logic

****** 1. Variables

Symbols like:

- $x$
- $y$
- $z$

These range over objects in a domain.

****** 2. Predicates

Instead of atomic propositions like $P$, we have predicates applied to terms:

- $\mathrm{Human}(x)$
- $\mathrm{Loves}(x,y)$

Predicates express properties and relations.

****** 3. Quantifiers

This is the major expansion.

- Universal quantifier:
  - $\forall x$
- Existential quantifier:
  - $\exists x$

Now we can express:

- $\forall x \, (\mathrm{Human}(x) \rightarrow \mathrm{Mortal}(x))$

This was impossible in propositional logic.

****** 4. Functions (optional but common)

Example:

- $\mathrm{Father}(x)$

Which allows nested structure:

- $\mathrm{Human}(\mathrm{Father}(x))$

**** What Extra Abilities Does First-Order Logic Bring?

***** Ability 1: Talk About Individuals

Propositional logic:

- $P$ : “Socrates is mortal.”

First-order logic:

- $\mathrm{Mortal}(\mathrm{Socrates})$

Now the internal structure is visible.

***** Ability 2: Express General Laws

Propositional logic must list cases:

- $P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3 \land \dots$

First-order logic expresses them compactly:

- $\forall x \, (\mathrm{Human}(x) \rightarrow \mathrm{Mortal}(x))$

***** Ability 3: Express Existence

Propositional logic cannot naturally express:

“There exists someone who loves everyone.”

First-order logic:

- $\exists x \, \forall y \, \mathrm{Loves}(x,y)$

***** Ability 4: Express Relations

Propositional logic cannot express:

“Everyone loves someone.”

First-order logic:

- $\forall x \, \exists y \, \mathrm{Loves}(x,y)$

***** Ability 5: Express Mathematical Structure

Arithmetic needs quantification:

- $\forall x \, (x + 0 = x)$

Propositional logic cannot express arithmetic laws.

**** Expressive Power Comparison

| Feature                 | Propositional Logic | First-Order Logic |
|-------------------------+---------------------+-------------------|
| Logical connectives     | Yes                 | Yes               |
| Variables               | No                  | Yes               |
| Predicates              | No                  | Yes               |
| Quantifiers             | No                  | Yes               |
| Relations               | No                  | Yes               |
| Arithmetic expressible  | No                  | Yes               |
| Talks about individuals | No                  | Yes               |

**** Cost of Extra Power

The extra expressive power comes with trade-offs:

- Propositional logic is decidable (truth tables always work).
- First-order logic is not decidable in general.
- First-order reasoning is more complex.

**** Conceptual Summary

- Propositional logic talks about whole facts.
- First-order logic talks about objects inside facts.

Propositional logic says:

“This statement is true.”

First-order logic says:

“For every object in the universe, this property holds.”

*** FOL Components

Back to FOL...

A first-order language is created by specifying:

1. Its symbols (vocabulary)
2. Rules for forming expressions (syntax)
3. Rules for assigning meaning (semantics)

Together, these define a complete formal language.

**** 1. Vocabulary (Alphabet of the Language)

To construct a first-order language, we first define its symbols.

***** A. Logical Symbols (Built-In)

These are common to all first-order languages:

- Quantifiers:
  - $\forall$ (for all)
  - $\exists$  (there exists)

- Logical connectives:
  - $\lnot$  (not)
  - $\land$  (and)
  - $\lor$  (or)
  - $\implies$  (implies)
  - $\iff$  (if and only if)

- Variables:
  - $x$, $y$, $z$, ...

- Equality (optional but common):
  - $=$

***** B. Non-Logical Symbols (Domain-Specific)

These depend on what the language is about.

1. Constant symbols (name specific objects)
   - $a$, $b$, $john$

2. Function symbols (map objects to objects)
   - $father(x)$
   - $plus(x, y)$

3. Predicate symbols (properties or relations)
   - ~Human(x)~
   - ~Loves(x, y)~
   - ~Prime(x)~

The choice of these symbols determines what the language can express.

**** 2. Syntax: Building Well-Formed Formulas

Syntax specifies how symbols may be combined.

***** Step 1: Terms

Terms refer to objects.

A term can be:
- A variable ($x$)
- A constant ($john$)
- A function applied to terms ($father(x)$, $plus(x, y)$)

***** Step 2: Atomic Formulas

An atomic formula is a predicate applied to terms.

Examples:
- ~Human(x)~
- ~Loves(john, mary)~
- ~Prime(7)~

***** Step 3: Complex Formulas

Formulas can be combined using logical connectives and quantifiers.

Examples:
- $\lnot Human(x)$
- $Human(x) \land Mortal(x)$
- $\forall x (Human(x) \implies Mortal(x))$
- $\exists x | Loves(x, mary)$

These formation rules define what counts as a grammatically correct
sentence.

**** 3. Semantics: Giving Meaning

Syntax defines structure.
Semantics defines meaning.

To interpret a first-order language, we specify:

1. A domain of discourse (the set of objects being discussed)
2. An interpretation that assigns:
   - Each constant → an object in the domain
   - Each function → a mapping over the domain
   - Each predicate → a relation over the domain

***** Example interpretation:

Domain: all people

Human(x): "x is a person"
Mortal(x): "x eventually dies"

Then the sentence:

  ∀x (Human(x) → Mortal(x))

means:

  For every object in the domain,
  if it is human, then it is mortal.

**** 4. How This Creates a Language

A first-order language is created by:

1. Defining a vocabulary
2. Defining formation rules (syntax)
3. Defining interpretation rules (semantics)

Comparison:

| Natural Language   | First-Order Logic |
|--------------------+-------------------|
| Words              | Symbols           |
| Grammar rules      | Formation rules   |
| Contextual meaning | Interpretation    |

First-order logic removes ambiguity by enforcing precise structure and meaning.

**** 5. Example: Constructing a Simple Language

Suppose we want a language about family relationships.

***** Vocabulary

- Constant: john
- Predicate: Parent(x, y)
- Predicate: Male(x)

***** Sentence

  ∀x (Parent(john, x) → Male(john))

Meaning:

  For every x,
  if john is a parent of x,
  then john is male.

This is a complete sentence in a first-order language.

**** 6. Why This Matters

First-order logic is used to construct:

- Mathematical theories
- Knowledge representation systems
- Database query systems
- Programming language semantics
- Artificial intelligence reasoning systems

It provides the structural foundation for precise formal reasoning.

**** Summary

First-order logic creates a language by providing:

1. Symbols (vocabulary)
2. Grammar rules (syntax)
3. Interpretation rules (semantics)

The result is a precise, unambiguous formal language capable of expressing structured reasoning about objects and their relationships.



*** Building the Language of Set Theory Using First-Order Logic

**** Overview

Here we walk through how first-order logic (FOL) is used to build the
*language of set theory*, step by step, with detailed examples.

Set theory (specifically Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory, ZF) is formulated
entirely inside a first-order language. What makes it powerful is that
*everything is a set* — numbers, functions, relations, ordered pairs —
all are defined using only sets.

We will follow these five steps:

1. Specify the vocabulary
2. Define terms
3. Define atomic formulas
4. Build complex formulas
5. Provide semantics (interpretation)

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Step 1: Specify the Vocabulary

A first-order language consists of logical symbols plus non-logical symbols.

***** Logical Symbols (standard in all FOL)

- Quantifiers: ∀, ∃
- Connectives: ¬, ∧, ∨, →, ↔
- Variables: x, y, z, x₁, x₂, ...
- Equality: =

***** Non-Logical Symbols for Set Theory

In standard ZF set theory, the language is extremely minimal.

It contains only:

- One binary predicate symbol: ∈  (membership)

That’s it.

There are:
- No constant symbols
- No function symbols
- No additional predicates

Everything must be expressed using:
  =
  ∈
  logical symbols

This simplicity is one of the remarkable features of set theory.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Step 2: Define Terms

In this language, terms are very simple.

Since there are:
- no constants
- no function symbols

The only terms are:

- Variables

Examples of terms:
- x
- y
- z
- x₁

That means every object referred to in the language is a variable.
Objects only gain meaning once interpreted in a model.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Step 3: Atomic Formulas

Atomic formulas are the simplest meaningful statements.

Because the only non-logical symbol is ∈,
the only atomic formulas are:

1. x ∈ y
2. x = y

Examples:

- x ∈ y
- y ∈ z
- x = y
- x ∈ x

Even something like:

  "x is a subset of y"

is not primitive. It must be defined using ∈:

  x ⊆ y  is shorthand for:

  ∀z (z ∈ x → z ∈ y)

So subset is not a new symbol —
it is a defined abbreviation inside the language.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Step 4: Build Complex Formulas

Now we use logical connectives and quantifiers
to build meaningful mathematical statements.

Here are important examples.

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 1: The Empty Set

We want to say:

  There exists a set with no elements.

Formal version:

  ∃x ∀y ¬(y ∈ x)

This says:

  There exists x such that
  for every y,
  y is not an element of x.

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 2: Subset Relation

Define:

  x ⊆ y

as:

  ∀z (z ∈ x → z ∈ y)

Now we can express:

  x = y  iff  x ⊆ y ∧ y ⊆ x

Which expands fully to:

  ∀z (z ∈ x ↔ z ∈ y)

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 3: Pairing Axiom

Informally:

  For any x and y,
  there exists a set containing exactly x and y.

Formal version:

  ∀x ∀y ∃z ∀w (w ∈ z ↔ (w = x ∨ w = y))

This expresses the existence of {x, y}.

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 4: Union Axiom

Informally:

  For any set x,
  there exists a set containing all elements of elements of x.

Formal version:

  ∀x ∃u ∀z (z ∈ u ↔ ∃y (y ∈ x ∧ z ∈ y))

This defines ⋃x.

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 5: Defining Ordered Pairs

Ordered pairs are not primitive.
They are defined using sets.

Kuratowski definition:

  (x, y) = {{x}, {x, y}}

Each part of this definition must itself
be built using earlier axioms (pairing, union, etc.).

So even ordered pairs reduce to pure membership structure.

--------------------------------------------------
***** Example 6: Defining Natural Numbers

In set theory:

0 = ∅
1 = {∅}
2 = {∅, {∅}}
3 = {∅, {∅}, {∅, {∅}}}
...

Each number is defined as:

  n = {0, 1, ..., n-1}

So arithmetic is encoded entirely using sets.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Step 5: Semantics (Interpretation)

To interpret this language, we specify:

1. A domain
2. An interpretation of ∈

In set theory, a model consists of:

- A collection M of objects
- A binary relation E on M

The formula:

  x ∈ y

means:

  x is related to y by E.

If the structure satisfies all axioms of ZF,
it is called a *model of set theory*.

Inside the intended model:

- The domain is the cumulative hierarchy of sets
- ∈ is actual set membership

But other, nonstandard models also exist.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** What Makes This Powerful?

With only:

  ∈
  =
  logical symbols

we can define:

- subsets
- unions
- intersections
- ordered pairs
- relations
- functions
- natural numbers
- real numbers
- metric spaces
- groups
- topological spaces

All of modern mathematics can be formalized
inside this tiny first-order language.

---------------------------------------------------------------------

**** Big Picture Summary

When first-order logic is applied to set theory:

1. The vocabulary is reduced to a single primitive relation (∈).
2. Terms are only variables.
3. Atomic formulas are membership or equality statements.
4. All mathematics is built using quantified combinations of those.
5. Meaning is given by a model consisting of a domain and a membership relation.

This shows how an entire mathematical universe
can be constructed from a minimal first-order language.
