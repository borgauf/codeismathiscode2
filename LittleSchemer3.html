Mathematical logic

If someone completely ignorant of math asks “What is math?” we might simply answer, “Math is the art of developing useful generalizations about specific situations.” If they then ask, “What do you mean by generalizations?” we could say “Generalization is the abstraction of specific situations into what they all have in common, i.e., general rules that apply to these many specific situations.” In any case, the whole philosophical “we observe and conclude” dynamic is what is behind mathematics … which makes math a form of philosophy. Chances are, however, that you have never experienced math in a philosophical way. Up until now it’s always been about “problem solving.” We mean to go further than mere problem solving.

But why do we need to abstract and generalize? Yes, we might need certain formul√¶ that let us get answers to, say, how much our mortgage payments should be. But do we need to go so deep into abstractions and generalization?

A story or two to that question. First Gottfried Leibnitz is considered by many to be the father of modern mathematical logic. He was the first to ask seriously if we could create a mathematically manipulatable calculus for human thought. But he also attempted to find a “real” definition of a straight line, saying the commonly accepted definition, namely, the shortest distance between two points, only described a property straight line. But by finding evermore fundamental abstractions, math gains in power, depth, and breadth.

A curious example of getting deeper and broader would be Einstein’s General Theory of Relativity, which took the abstraction of gravity to previously unimaginable heights. Before Einstein, physics relied on Issac Newton’s all but complete computational model of gravity as a real force (\(F\))

\begin{align*} F = G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2} \end{align*}

On the other hand, Einstein’s General Relativity described gravity as an “artifact of four-dimensional space-time geometry.” Physicists today are unanimous in considering Einstein’s explanation of gravity the superior, overarching, “meta” view, even if gravity has been seemingly downgraded to an “artifact.”

Abstraction uses symbols. If we wanted to depict a person’s life as a set of key events, we could paint an elaborate mural on a huge wall showing various scenes from that person’s life. Or we could just do this

\begin{align*} L_{P} = \{E_1, E_2, E_3, \ldots E_n \} \end{align*}

where \(E_k\) is a key event. Obviously, this would not be a very colorful, let alone descriptive depiction of a life; but if we represent a life as a set of event elements, perhaps we could develop a method for each of the elements to carry more information—and now we’re entering the world of data management and math. Important is how we have reduced and generalized to a set. And as we’ve noted before, modern mathematics wants to ground so much of itself in set theory. Peano axiomatizes mathematics

So-called higher math wants to ground math in logically unassailable, fundamental, formalistic, foundational bedrock. This became a serious pursuit of math starting in the late nineteenth century. Giuseppe Peano put forth his Peano Axioms in 1889, which became that bedrock upn which all math could be built.

Propositional logic

The term propositional logic (PL) contains the root proposition, which is a statement that can be evaluated to be true or false, as well as how these statements may be combined.

PL is the most basic form of logic, started mainly by the Greeks, largely centered around Aristotle, literally to sort out thinking. Fast forward to the nineteenth century when the English mathematician George Boole in his books The Mathematical Analysis of Logic and The Laws of Thought went about modernizing Aristotelian logic. Boole’s attempt to answer the existential question, Can we formalize, mathematize human thought? resulted in Boolean algebra, which, among other things, became the basis of digital computer circuitry.

Implication

We want to discuss the if-then-else sexp we saw before in more depth. An if statement is a programming application of the general math concept of the implication.

Consider the conditional statement If it snows, the snowplows will come out. This is also contains, i.e., something is being implied. The symbol used for implication is a right-pointing arrow between the antecedent (or hypothesis) and the consequent (or conclusion) like this

\begin{align*} p \longrightarrow q \end{align*}

and can be phrased in many different ways

  • \(p\) implies \(q\)
  • if \(p\) then \(q\)
  • \(p\) only if \(q\)
  • \(q\) if \(p\)
  • all \(p\) are \(q\)
  • if \(p\), \(q\)
  • whenever \(p\) is true, \(q\) is true
  • \(q\) is true whenever \(p\) is true
  • \(q\) is necessary for \(p\) (or, if \(p\) is true, then \(q\) is necessarily true)
  • \(p\) is sufficient for \(q\) (or, for \(q\) to be true, it is sufficient to show \(p\) is true)

Let’s use these in normal sentences

Proofs in mathematics are typically based around implications. Here is an axiomatic truth about integers

The integer \(n\) is even if there exists an integer \(k\) such that \(n = 2k\), and \(n\) is odd if there exists an integer \(k\) such that \(n = 2k + 1\). Note that every integer is either even or odd, and no integer is both even and odd. Two integers have the same parity when both are even or both are odd; they have opposite parity when one is even and the other is odd.

Now, a direct proof of any sort of conditional statement of the form \(p \longrightarrow q\) will rely on the truth of implication. A theorem we need to prove as valid will typically start by assuming the \(p\) part, the hypothesis, then use axioms, definitions, givens, as well as previously proven theorems to prove the \(q\) part must also be true. Syllogisms

Syllogisms are a typical starting point with logic. The classic Aristotelian syllogism goes like this

  • Major premise: All humans are mortal.
  • Minor premise: All Greeks are humans.
  • Conclusion/Consequent: All Greeks are mortal.

Why not abstract this?1 The symbol \(\therefore\) means therefore.

\begin{align*} &\text{All H are M}& \\ &\underline{\text{All G are H}}& \\ \therefore\: &\text{All G are M}& \end{align*}

Again, we see symbols (letters) standing in for specific things. But obviously we can’t just stick anything in for the letter symbols. False premises will only get us false conclusions.

\begin{align*} &\text{All fish are towns.}& \\ &\underline{\text{All guppies are fish.}}& \\ \therefore\: &\text{All guppies are towns.}& \end{align*}

This would only make sense if all fish are towns.

Hypothetical syllogisms

As a part of the hypothetical syllogism is a variation of syllogisms where conditional statements are made.

The pure hypothetical syllogism looks like this

\begin{align*} &p \longrightarrow q& \\ &\underline{q \longrightarrow r}& \\ \therefore\: &p \longrightarrow r& \end{align*}
