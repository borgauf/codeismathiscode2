** Basics of graph theory

In its most basic form a *graph* is nothing more than points and lines
connecting these points. But of course the whole purpose of these
points and connecting lines is to represent concepts. Below we see a
section of a /thematic/ map of the London subway system.

#+begin_figure
#+CAPTION: Section of the London subway system thematic map
[[file:images/LondonUnderground1.png]]
#+end_figure

Being "thematic" means we aren't supposed to see this map as a true
and accurate map as the roughly same area depicted below

#+begin_figure
#+CAPTION: Section of Google Maps London
[[file:images/GoogleMapsLondon1.png]]
#+end_figure

Instead, the London Underground map is meant to display only the
relevant information of

- subway stops
- relative positions of the subway stops
- rail lines running between subway stops

with no consideration of further accuracy or usage. Obviously, we're
in the realm of abstraction when we boil down a regular, reasonably
accurate map into one of just points and lines. And yet even small
children can grasp what is meant by Figure 1. Below is a thematic map
depiction of the famous /[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Bridges_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg][Seven Bridges of Königsberg]]/, where graph
theory began as the Swiss mathematician Leonard Euler first abstracted
the idea of bridges and land masses (two river banks and two islands
in a river) down to just points and lines.

#+begin_figure
#+CAPTION: Euler's seven bridges of Königsberg
[[file:images/eulergraphbridges.png]]
#+end_figure

@@html:<font color = "#650d1c">@@
A graph is an ordered pair $G = (V, E)$ where $V$ is the set of vertices, and $E ⊆
\{{x, y\} \; \vert \; x, y \in V, x \ne y}$ is a set of unordered pairs of vertices called edges.
